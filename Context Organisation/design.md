# Code Archive 2 Design

## Mission

`Code Archive 2` is not a backup, mirror, or cleanup pass.

It is a new knowledge system built from the archived material in `Code Archive`.

Its goals are:

1. Find the most durable organization that emerges from your actual investigation patterns.
2. Preserve only information that strengthens long-term conceptual understanding, operational capability, or research identity.
3. Produce notes that are readable by both future-you and external systems such as OpenClaw.

The original archive remains untouched and authoritative as historical storage.
`Code Archive 2` becomes the curated intelligence layer.

---

## What The Archive Actually Contains

After reading the folder structure and representative notes, the archive is not primarily about "coding" in the narrow sense.
It is a mixed research environment centered on:

- agentic systems and digital workers
- tool evaluation and platform comparison
- workflow and operating-model design
- active project experiments
- core technical foundations
- semantic and philosophical synthesis
- strategic judgment about what to trust, avoid, or pursue

The strongest recurring pattern is not language-specific programming.
The strongest recurring pattern is the design of a personal research-and-action system built around AI agents, tools, and knowledge infrastructure.

That means the new structure should reflect your real mode of inquiry:

- how systems work
- how tools compare
- how agents are organized
- how projects operationalize ideas
- how concepts connect into a larger worldview

## Design Principles

### 1. Preserve Signal, Not Volume

A note survives only if it does at least one of these:

- clarifies a durable concept
- explains an operational method you can actually reuse
- captures a high-value comparison or judgment
- documents an active architecture worth extending
- strengthens the philosophical or semantic backbone of the wider investigation

Interesting but disposable notes do not belong here.

### 2. Organize By Use, Not By Source

The old archive often groups by where information came from or when it was collected.
The new archive should group by how the knowledge functions in your system.

### 3. Keep Top-Level Folders Few And Stable

Top-level categories should survive changing tools and changing projects.
A durable archive is shallow at the top and rich in summary notes.

### 4. Prefer Hubs Over Piles

Each major subject area should have hub notes that explain:

- what the subject is
- why it matters
- what subtopics exist
- what the current stance is
- what links to what

### 5. Separate Timeless Concepts From Fast-Changing Intelligence

A note about "what an API is" ages slowly.
A note about "best free model in April 2026" ages quickly.
Both may be useful, but they should not live in the same conceptual layer without distinction.

### 6. Write For Transfer

The notes must be usable by a human reader and legible to OpenClaw.
That means:

- strong titles
- clear summaries
- explicit reasoning
- low ambiguity
- stable terminology

## Why This Structure Fits Your Usage Pattern

The old archive suggests you investigate subjects through a recurring loop:

1. Notice a capability, tool, or conceptual opening.
2. Compare and evaluate the surrounding ecosystem.
3. Connect it to a broader operating model.
4. Test it through a project or experimental architecture.
5. Fold the useful part into a larger conceptual framework.
6. Develop a judgment about what is hype, what is durable, and what is unsafe.

The proposed structure mirrors that loop.

It is not a standard computer science taxonomy.
It is a map of your research behavior.

## Survival Criteria

Each note from the old archive will be judged with one of these outcomes:

### `Survive As Distilled Core`

The subject is strong and deserves a rewritten note in `Code Archive 2`.

Use when:

- the idea is durable
- the reasoning is strong
- the topic is central to your long-term investigation

### `Survive As Operational Brief`

The note is time-sensitive but still useful because it contains concrete, deployable guidance.

Use when:

- a tool comparison leads to action
- a configuration is immediately usable
- a project brief is worth keeping in compact form

### `Merge Into Subject Hub`

The original note is too small, too overlapping, or too narrow to survive alone, but its information should be absorbed into a larger subject file.

This will likely be the most common outcome.

### `Discard From Code Archive 2`

The note may remain in the original archive but will not be carried into the curated system.

Use when:

- it is repetitive
- it is vague
- it is mostly hype language without durable content
- it does not affect your long-term research direction
- it does not teach, operationalize, or clarify anything substantial

## Note Types To Create In The New System

The new archive should not be a flat pile of rewritten notes.
It should use a small set of note types consistently.

### Subject Hub

A central note for a major topic.

Structure:

- subject definition
- why it matters
- major subtopics
- current stance
- related notes

### Distilled Brief

A compact, high-signal note derived from several overlapping notes.

Structure:

- core claim
- why it matters
- what is true
- what is uncertain
- what to do with it

### Concept Note

A durable explanation of a foundational idea.

Structure:

- plain explanation
- role in the bigger system
- examples
- related concepts

### Project Lens

A project summary written to extract reusable value.

Structure:

- project purpose
- architecture
- what was learned
- what remains valuable
- what should be reused elsewhere

### Filter Note

A cautionary or evaluative note.

Structure:

- what the issue is
- why it matters
- practical implication
- recommendation

## Naming Rules

The original archive has significant naming drift:

- inconsistent capitalization
- emoji-heavy titles
- mixed conceptual and conversational naming
- dates or hype phrases embedded in titles
- near-duplicate subjects spread across multiple folders

`Code Archive 2` should standardize names with these rules:

1. Use plain-language titles.
2. Avoid emojis in final file names.
3. Prefer durable titles over momentary phrasing.
4. If a note is time-sensitive, include the date only when it materially matters.
5. Name files by subject, not by emotional framing.

Examples:

- `What Is OpenClaw.md`
- `Dynamic Model Routing.md`
- `Tool Evaluation Criteria.md`
- `CognitiveEngine Project Lens.md`
- `Semantic Architecture Overview.md`
